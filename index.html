The Disappearing American Farm

The number of US farms peaked at 6.8 million in 1935. Today fewer than 2 million remain, while the average farm has tripled in size.

Number of US Farms

Millions of farms, 1900–2024

Average Farm Size

Acres per farm, 1900–2024

How is a "farm" counted? The USDA defines a farm as any place that produced and sold — or normally would have sold — at least $1,000 of agricultural products during the year. This threshold has been used since 1974; earlier censuses used lower cutoffs, meaning some pre-1974 counts include very small homesteads that wouldn't qualify today. Average farm size is total land in farms (owned and rented) divided by total number of farms.
Fewer farms, bigger farms. America lost nearly 5 million farms between 1935 and today. The average farm tripled in size, from about 155 acres to 466. The largest 4% of farms now work more than half of all US cropland.

Dairy: From Every Farm to Factory Scale

In 1940, milking a few cows was just part of farming — 4.6 million farms reported milk cows. By 2022, only 24,094 specialized operations sold milk. Yet America produces more milk today than it did then.

Number of US Dairy Operations

Farms with milk cows / licensed dairy operations, 1940–2022

What counts as a dairy operation? The definition changed over time, which is why the early numbers are so large. In 1940, the Census counted any farm that reported milking one or more cows — including farms where a family kept a single cow for household milk. The 2022 number (24,094) counts farms that actually sold milk during the census year. Both counts are accurate for their definitions; the shift reflects how dairy went from a near-universal farm sideline to a specialized commercial industry.
From 4.6 million to 24,094. In 1940, most farms kept a handful of milk cows. Today, dairy is a specialized industry: the remaining 24,094 operations average over 300 cows each, and the largest 3% produce more than half of all US milk. Total production: 117 billion lbs (1944) → 226 billion lbs (2022).

Hog Farming: From Barnyard to Factory

In 1920, keeping a few hogs was standard on nearly every farm — 4.9 million farms reported raising them. By 2022, hog production had consolidated into just 56,000 specialized operations.

Number of US Hog Operations

Farms raising hogs, 1920–2022

What counts as a hog operation? The 1920 figure counts any farm that reported raising one or more hogs or pigs. On a 1920s farm, keeping a few pigs for slaughter was standard practice — not a specialized business. The 2022 Census counts farms that produced and sold hogs. As with dairy, the decline reflects a real structural change: what was once part of everyday farming is now an industrial operation.
Bigger operations, fewer farmers. In 1920, raising hogs was a sideline on most farms. Today it's an industrial operation: 73% of all hogs are on farms with 5,000+ animals, and the top 4 pork packers control 67% of slaughter (up from 34% in 1980).

Meatpacking: Four Companies, 85% of Beef

In 1977, the top 4 beef packers handled just 25% of slaughter. By 2019, four companies — Tyson, JBS, Cargill, and National Beef — controlled 85%.

Beef Packing CR4

Top 4 firms' combined share of US cattle slaughter (CR4)

Meat Industry CR4 Comparison

Beef, pork, and broiler processing concentration

How is CR4 measured here? The beef CR4 is the share of total federally inspected steer and heifer slaughter (by head count) handled by the four largest packing firms, using USDA Food Safety and Inspection Service (FSIS) plant-level data. Steer and heifer slaughter is used because it represents the commercial fed-cattle market — the main beef supply chain. Cull cows and bulls (sold from dairy/breeding herds) are excluded. Pork and broiler CR4 are measured the same way: share of total federally inspected slaughter by head count.
Most of the consolidation happened in one decade. A merger wave in the 1980s (IBP, ConAgra, Excel/Cargill) took the top 4's share from 36% to 71% in just 12 years. By the mid-1990s they held over 80% — and have stayed there since.

Poultry: The Company Owns the Chicken

In chicken farming, companies like Tyson own the birds, the feed, and the processing plants. Farmers provide the labor and the land — and carry the debt.

Broiler Processing CR4

Top 4 firms' share of broiler slaughter, 1980–2022

Top Broiler Companies (2020)

Market share of leading broiler processors

What is a "broiler"? A chicken raised for meat (as opposed to egg-laying hens). Market share here is measured by share of total birds slaughtered at federally inspected plants. "Vertical integration" means a single company controls the entire chain: breeding, hatching, feed production, growing, slaughter, and processing. The farmer's role is limited to raising the company's birds in the company's way.
Farmers don't own what they raise. A chicken farmer may invest over $1 million in specialized barns but never owns the birds inside them. The company delivers chicks, feed, and medicine; the farmer provides land and labor. The top 10 companies control about 80% of all US chicken.

Grocery: Where You Shop Is Decided for You

In the average state, the top 4 grocery chains control 67% of sales — up from 43% in 2000. Walmart alone takes about $1 of every $4 spent on groceries.

State-Level Grocery CR4

Average top-4 market share across US states

National Grocery Market Shares (2024)

Estimated share of US grocery sales

How is grocery concentration measured? The state-level CR4 is the combined dollar sales of the four largest grocery parent companies in each state, averaged across all states (from RAFI USA's Grocery Gap Atlas, built with University of Chicago). National market shares are estimated from total US grocery revenue. "Grocery" here includes supermarkets, supercenters, and warehouse clubs — not convenience stores or restaurants.
Supercenters replaced local grocers. The number of supercenters (Walmart, Costco) grew 350% between 1990 and 2015, while independent grocery stores steadily disappeared.

Seeds & Chemicals: Three Mergers Changed Everything

Between 2015 and 2018, three mega-mergers worth over $230 billion turned six agrochemical giants into four. A handful of companies now control most of the seeds and chemicals farmers need to grow food.

How is seed/chemical concentration measured? Seed CR4 is the top 4 firms' share of global commercial seed sales by revenue (USDA ERS). Agrochemical CR4 is the same for pesticides, herbicides, and fungicides by global revenue. These are global figures because the seed and chemical markets are dominated by multinational companies. The US corn/soybean figures (72% / 66%) measure share of planted acres using seed from each company, per USDA survey data.
Two companies supply most of America's crop seeds. Corteva and Bayer together account for 72% of US corn seed, 66% of soybeans, and over 50% of cotton seed (2018–2020, USDA ERS).

About the Data

All data comes from US government sources: the USDA Census of Agriculture (conducted every 5 years), USDA Economic Research Service (ERS), USDA NASS, and USDA-funded peer-reviewed research. Each section includes definitions of what's being measured, links to original sources, and notes on what the numbers do and don't include.

What is CR4? The combined market share of the four largest companies in an industry. Economists generally consider a CR4 above 40% "highly concentrated" and above 60% an oligopoly. For reference: US beef packing is at 85%.

Some values are rounded (indicated with ~). Post-2018 seed/chemical CR4 values are estimates because required divestitures complicate exact calculation.
